Rules

Overview: Order in the Court is a story-building improv game where players assume the roles of various people in a court case and argue to reveal the truth using witnesses, evidence, and wit. The rules are fairly loose and can be changed depending on how your group would like to play.

Players: 3+

Roles

THE JUDGE: The judge calls the shots. It is up to the judge to decide what questions to ask, who gets to talk, who is successful at making their case, and when to introduce witnesses. They can decide whether they'd like to keep score based on evidence / witness impressions or whether they'd rather leave the scoring more loose and pick a winner at the end.

THE PLAINTIFF / THE DEFENDANT: The plaintiff has filed a law suit against the defendant over a certain ISSUE. Both sides will make their case in a handful of ways:

  1. EVIDENCE: Each side (P & D) has some amount of evidence. They can use this evidence to prove their case - if they can find ways to connect their evidence to their argument.
  2. WITNESSES: In a game of at least 4 people, witnesses may be brought into court. Witnesses can stay neutral or pick a side, but must find a way to connect their identity to the case at hand, offering some sort of insight. The judge picks when to call witnesses and which witness to call. The judge and the two sides (P & D) can ask the witness questions, and the judge decides what to believe.
  3. WIT: When the floor is yours to speak, you can make your case however you want! It is best to stick to evidence and witnesses, but any other points are also worth making (especially puns).
